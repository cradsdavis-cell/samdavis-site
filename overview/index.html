Overview

What an AI that knows you actually does.

§ It knows me

Context, not prompts.

A generic AI assistant is a stranger you brief every time. The thing I build instead is a long-term context layer: who you are, what you care about right now, what you're trying to build, and the eight layers of knowledge that change rarely. When you ask it something, it doesn't start from zero. It starts from a year of you.

The layers are deliberate — North Star, Philosophy, Self, Network, Past, Goals, Tasks, Workflow. Each one is a single source of truth. The assistant pulls from them when relevant. You don't paste your bio into every prompt.

§ It knows my people

A network, tiered.

Every person who matters has a page — what you've talked about, what they're working on, how often you should be in touch, what the next ask might be. Tiered: core (family + chosen-family), live (active threads), warm (alive but slower), dormant, stub.

When a name comes up in conversation, the assistant already knows the relationship history. It catches when you haven't pinged someone in two weeks and they're a "live" thread. It drafts outreach in your voice, with the right context, at the right altitude for the relationship.

§ It writes in my voice

Concrete. Vulnerable. Yours.

Generic AI output is detectable in three seconds — em-dashes everywhere, "delve into" everything, every sentence the same length. I build a voice profile from your actual writing: signature phrases, sentence rhythms, the things you don't say. The assistant runs every outbound draft through a seven-check audit before it shows you anything.

When a draft sounds like a LinkedIn post, it gets flagged. When it crosses into corporate-fluent register, it gets rewritten. The first draft you see already sounds like you.

§ It catches drift

Working memory, not a filing cabinet.

The assistant updates the wiki as conversation surfaces material — a new person mentioned, a decision stated, a priority shift. These trigger silent edits: a person page gets bumped, a decision lands in the log, a stale fact gets flagged for review.

A daily lint catches drift — schema violations, stale pages, broken references. A weekly sync reconciles your goals against the active projects. Coherence isn't something you maintain. It's something the system surfaces when it slips.

§ It uses the right tool for the job

Skills + connectors.

The assistant has named skills — short workflows for specific tasks. Open a session. Outreach. Draft a follow-up. Audit the inbox. Each one is a small set of steps that knows when to fire, what tools to use, and what to write back.

It connects to Gmail, Calendar, Todoist, WhatsApp, GitHub, Drive — whatever your operational surface actually is. The assistant lives in the tools you already use, not in a new app you have to remember to open.

§ Case study · Carbon Tracker

Eleven agents. Six weeks.

A UK climate consultancy was spending three days per client triaging Scope-3 carbon-accounting questions across multiple frameworks. The framework-mapping was rote, the cross-referencing was painful, and every analyst had a slightly different opinion about how it should be done.

I designed an eleven-agent pipeline that triages incoming questions, decides which carbon framework applies, cross-checks against the consultancy's historical decisions, and surfaces an auditable chain of reasoning. Each agent has one job. Each handoff is logged. The output is a document the analyst reviews and signs off, not replaces.

Time-to-answer dropped from three days to under twenty minutes. The system shipped in around six weeks. The consultancy now uses it as the first-pass on every client engagement.
